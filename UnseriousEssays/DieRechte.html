Since the dissolution of the Nazi Party in 1945, Germany has witnessed a significant rise in far-right extremist groups fueled by nationalist sentiments, anti-immigrant rhetoric, and xenophobia. The trial of the National Socialist Underground (NSU), a neo-Nazi terrorist organization, shed light on the extent of far-right activities and the failures of German security agencies to address the threat. Some responses the German government has implemented include the NetzDG law, which aims to combat the spread of hate speech and illegal content online while strengthening law enforcement efforts and investing in programs to prevent radicalization and promote social integration.

After the end of the war in Germany, in May of 1945, the Allied Powers France, the UK, the US, and the Soviet Union divided Germany into four occupation zones and started the process of
  denazification
  demilitarization
  decentralization
  democratization
also referred to as the 4Ds

To denazification the country, NSDAP (the Nazi party) and all sub-organizations were banned, its laws were abolished, and all signs of the Third Reich were erased from everyday life, including books, uniforms, medals, and street names.

Germany also had to make reparation payments to the Victorious powers and other affected countries that were mainly paid in Machinery manufacturing plants and forced labor. 

Most Germans were also subject to investigations by the International Military Tribunal that was looking to identify the different roles people played in the Holocaust and in committing war crimes.

Germans were divided into five categories, from major offenders to exonerated individuals. Major offenders were tried in the first Nuremberg Trial from November 1945 to October 1946 and in 12 subsequent Nuremberg trials that were solely held by the U.S. Of all 36 defendants were sentenced to death, 125 received prison sentences, 23 of them for life, not all of these verdicts were actually executed though a lot of the convicts ended up being released after just a few years and pardoned from the death penalty as the denazification process became more and more lenient in the western zones.

Due to the extremely high number of cases, less severe offenders were soon handed over to civilian tribunals in Bayreuth under German Administration, and it became increasingly difficult to find a balance between punishment and rebuilding the country for which the occupying Powers needed to fill a lot of important positions and having hundreds of thousands of Germans in internment camps didn't make that easy.

But with the development of the Cold War, West Germany was considered to become an important Ally. The focus quickly started shifting away from strict denazification and more toward Rehabilitation.

By 1948 countless trials were seized or never started, and lots of people were pardoned. This, to this day, is heavily criticized as it meant that people that used to be active members of the Nazi party ended up keeping their high-ranking jobs and remaining in positions of power in the newly founded West German Republic.

In the Soviet occupation zone, denazification was followed through a lot more strictly and quickly. By the Spring of 1948, they had fired and replaced over 500,000 people.

In view of a general "shift to the right" in German society, as evidenced first by the mobilization successes of Pegida and then by the AfD's entry into all state parliaments and the Bundestag, the phrase "new right" is once again gaining wider currency.
"New Right" stands for a group of intellectuals that is mainly based on the ideas of the Conservative Revolution of the Weimar Republic, is more of a network without fixed organizational structures, and wants to drive fundamental political change with a "cultural revolution from the right.



In terms of ideology, one refers to the Conservative Revolution (the "Young Conservatives") in the Weimar Republic, which was also an intellectual current. This ambivalent designation meant that they no longer wanted to preserve what existed but to overcome it—the Weimar Republic was a democratic constitutional state. In contrast, supposedly lost values were to be revived: Elite, leadership, God, nation, nature, order, race, and Volksgemeinschaft. None of this had anything to do with enlightenment, equality, the principle of individuality, liberalism, human rights, parliamentarism, party democracy, or pluralism. The alternative was to be an authoritarian dictatorship with a real mass base.



The best-known defenders of the Conservative Revolution were the speechwriter Edgar Julius Jung, the publicist Arthur Moeller van den Bruck, the constitutionalist Carl Schmitt, and the cultural philosopher Oswald Spengler. Today's New Right invokes these anti-republican thinkers.



There is no fixed organizational structure, such as a party or an association for them. Actually: one cannot become a formal member of the New Right and receive a membership card. Rather, it is a network of different publicists: people listen to each other, they know each other, they read each other's texts, and they meet.
There is ideological common ground with regard to the appeal to the Conservative Revolution. Nevertheless, there are also differences on certain issues: Do they appeal to Christianity, or do they oppose it? Should there be more market economy or more welfare state? Does one want to pursue fundamental opposition or promote party politics? A more solid form of organization would also be unlikely in view of such differences.
Thus, there is rather a loose and personal contact, which is also articulated in simple joint publishing and lecturing. For this purpose, anthologies and theoretical organs, as well as conferences and seminars, can serve as forums.

What the New Right has in common is that it advocates an intellectual overcoming of the democratic constitutional state. Terms such as "battle for minds," "cultural revolution from the right" or "meta-politics" are chosen for this.
What is meant is the following basic assumption: intellectual change must precede political change. First, one's own positions must gain hegemony in discourse, and only then can they be translated into politics: Italian Marxist Antonio Gramsci- who called for an intellectual revolution for the communists as a prerequisite for a political revolution. This strategic approach was then adopted by the New Right with different ideological overtones.
In this respect, the New Right relies less on political movements and parties, seeing itself more as their mastermind. Nevertheless, their representatives also approach such forms of politics, depending on the situation.
